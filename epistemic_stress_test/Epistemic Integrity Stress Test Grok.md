 Combinatorics in the Context of Probability

## Phase 1: Main Concept Analysis

Combinatorics constitutes the mathematical foundation for systematically and rigorously counting the possible configurations within a finite set. In the context of probability theory, as outlined in the Course Notes by Grotto, Maurelli, and Pratelli, it allows the assignment of probabilities in uniform models, where every elementary outcome is equally likely.

A uniform model on a finite sample space $\Omega$ is defined when $\mathcal{F} = \mathcal{P}(\Omega)$ and $P(\{\omega\}) = \frac{1}{|\Omega|}$ for every $\omega \in \Omega$. In this case, for any event $A \subseteq \Omega$:

$$P(A) = \frac{|A|}{|\Omega|} = \frac{\text{"favorable outcomes"}}{\text{"possible outcomes"}}$$

This classical interpretation of probability (Laplace) relies entirely on combinatorics and is particularly effective for experiments involving urns, dice rolls, card games, or sampling. Combinatorics translates counting problems into closed-form equations, bypassing the need for exhaustive enumeration.

### Phase 2: Theoretical and Mathematical Development

The three fundamental formulas presented in the text are:

1. **Ordered sequences with repetition** (functions from a set of $k$ elements to a set of $n$ elements):
   $$n^k$$

2. **Permutations** (orderings of $n$ distinct elements):
   $$n! = n \times (n-1) \times \dots \times 1$$
   *(with $0! = 1$ by convention).*

3. **Binomial coefficient** (selection of $k$ elements out of $n$ without order and without repetition):
   $$\binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!} = \frac{n \times (n-1) \times \dots \times (n-k+1)}{k!}$$

These are derived from fundamental multiplication principles and inductive reasoning. The binomial coefficient appears directly in Newton's binomial theorem:

$$(a + b)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} a^k b^{n-k}$$

### Inductive Proof of the Binomial Coefficient (Sketch)
The proof proceeds by induction on $n$. The base case $n=1$ is trivial. The inductive step utilizes Pascal's identity:

$$\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n-1}{k} + \binom{n-1}{k-1}$$

### Application Examples (Textual and Extended)
* **Sampling with replacement** ($k$ ordered selections from $n$ objects): $\Omega$ has a cardinality of $n^k$ under a uniform probability distribution.
* **Sampling without replacement** ($k \leq n$): Cardinality is given by $P(n,k) = \frac{n!}{(n-k)!}$ (partial permutations).
* **Unordered sampling** (combinations): $\binom{n}{k}$.

**Quantitative Example:** An urn contains 10 marbles. We draw 3 marbles without replacement and without order.
The total number of possible outcomes is: $\binom{10}{3} = 120$.

If 4 of the marbles are red, the probability of drawing exactly 2 red marbles is:

$$\frac{\binom{4}{2} \binom{6}{1}}{120} = \frac{6 \times 6}{120} = 0.3$$

### Critical Comparison of Approaches
* **Classical (Laplace):** Intuitive but limited to finite and uniform spaces. It does not easily handle dependencies or continuous sample spaces.
* **Frequentist:** Interprets probability as the limit of relative frequencies as $n \to \infty$. Combinatorics provides the exact numerator for finite samples.
* **Axiomatic (Kolmogorov):** Generalizes all models, yet combinatorics remains the primary computational tool in finite discrete scenarios.

**Limitations:** Combinatorial explosion ($n!$ grows faster than exponentially) makes direct calculations impractical for large $n$. In such cases, one must rely on approximations (such as Stirling's approximation: $n! \approx \sqrt{2\pi n} \left(\frac{n}{e}\right)^n$) or Monte Carlo simulations.
