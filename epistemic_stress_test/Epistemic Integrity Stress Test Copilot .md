## Epistemic Integrity Stress Test

### 1 Purpose of the Test

The purpose of this stress test is to evaluate the epistemic robustness of a text generated by a closed autoregressive model (copilot). The text produced is subjected to external validation (MarCognity-AI) in order to make the 'fracture' between linguistic coherence and epistemic foundation observable, identifying the residual regime of uncertainty (Epistemic Boundary) that remains despite the apparent authoritativeness of the tone.
The goal is not to compare models, but:
Glottodidactic content is not evaluated.
The analysis concerns exclusively the epistemic structure of the text.

The procedure is fully reproducible and can be replicated with any closed autoregressive model and any independent external validator.

### 1. General Introduction
Acquisitional didactics represents one of today's most advanced attempts to integrate Second Language Acquisition (SLA) research with teaching practice. Its goal is not simply to "teach better," but to harmonize educational intervention with natural acquisition processes, recognizing that:

> "Students do not learn exactly what the teacher teaches and/or at the moment they teach it."
> *(translated from the provided document)*

This seemingly trivial observation has profound implications:

* Language learning follows natural sequences.
* Certain structures can only be taught at specific stages.
* Other structures, despite appearing "late" in the acquisition sequences, are communicatively indispensable.

The paper by Nuzzo & Rastelli addresses precisely this core issue: how to pedagogically treat linguistic politeness—a complex, late-acquired, and socio-culturally sensitive domain.

---

### 2. Phase 1 – Problem Analysis: Fundamental Concepts

### 2.1. What is Acquisitional Didactics?
Acquisitional didactics is defined as:

* A research program that applies the experimental method to language teaching.
* A bridge between acquisitional linguistics (which studies spontaneous learning) and glottodidactics (which designs educational interventions).
* An approach that aims to respect acquisition sequences, avoiding the teaching of what the learner cannot yet acquire.

It is based on three main assumptions:

1.  Natural acquisition sequences exist (Pienemann, 1998; Giacalone Ramat, 2003).
2.  Teaching can facilitate, but not force, the transition from one stage to another.
3.  Guided and spontaneous learning share cognitive mechanisms but differ in terms of input, feedback, and noticing.

### 2.2. The Core Issue of Linguistic Politeness
Linguistic politeness in Italian L2 involves:

* **Pragmalinguistics:** Linguistic forms (*TU/LEI*, imperatives, clitics, greeting formulas).
* **Sociopragmatics:** Social norms, roles, distance, power, and context.

The paper highlights that:

> "Pronominal address constitutes one of the least transparent sub-systems of the entire Italian inflectional system"

and that its acquisition is late, unstable, and highly variable.

---

### 3. Phase 2 – Theoretical Development and Comparison of Models

### 3.1. Acquisition Sequences
According to Processability Theory (Pienemann, 1998), a learner can only acquire a structure when their interlanguage system is ready to process it. This implies that:

* Clitic pronouns,
* The formal imperative,
* Gender agreement in the *LEI* form,
* Forms of address,

are structures of high morphosyntactic complexity $\rightarrow$ and are therefore late-acquired.

### General Processability Formula
The theory can be formalized as:

$$\text{Acquisition Possible} \iff \text{Processability Level} \ge \text{Structural Complexity}$$

For politeness:

$$\text{Complexity (LEI)} = \text{gender agreement} + \text{clitics} + \text{imperative} + \text{sociopragmatic norms}$$

$\rightarrow$ This results in a structure of extremely high complexity.

### 3.2. Interlanguage Pragmatics
According to Kasper & Blum-Kulka (1993), pragmatic acquisition requires:

* Rich and authentic input,
* Metapragmatic awareness,
* Opportunities for meaning negotiation.

However, the paper shows that native speakers tend to use a pragmatically simplified *foreigner talk* with non-natives, reducing:

* The use of *LEI*,
* Politeness formulas,
* Mitigators/attenuators.

This produces distorted input, which can lead to:

* Fossilization,
* Perceptions of discrimination,
* Difficulties in social integration.

### 3.3. Comparison of Theoretical Models

| Model | Advantages | Limitations |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Processability Theory** | Predicts sequences; highly useful for morphosyntax. | Limited applicability to pragmatics. |
| **Politeness Theory** (Brown & Levinson) | Universal; effectively distinguishes between positive and negative face. | Criticized for ethnocentrism. |
| **Variable Sociopragmatics** (Molinelli, Liddicoat) | Considers context and local identity. | Difficult to formalize. |
| **Input Interaction Hypothesis** (Long) | Highlights the crucial role of feedback. | Does not explain social norms. |

The paper by Nuzzo & Rastelli is situated at an intersection: how can we pedagogically treat a domain that is simultaneously morphosyntactic and socio-cultural?

### 5. Phase 4 – Critical Evaluation of the Paper

### 5.1. Strengths
* **Originality:** It addresses an under-researched topic (politeness as a testing ground for acquisitional didactics).
* **Theoretical Solidity:** It integrates acquisitional linguistics, pragmatics, and glottodidactics.
* **Social Relevance:** It connects pragmatic simplification to dynamics of discrimination.
* **Innovative Pedagogical Proposal:** Introducing politeness right from the very first teaching units.

### 5.2. Methodological Limitations
* **Absence of robust experimental data:** Examples are anecdotal or qualitative; a controlled experimental design is missing.
* **Lack of longitudinal measurements:** The evolution of the interlanguage over time is not observed.
* **Undefined sample:** The number, age, L1, and proficiency level of the learners are not specified.
* **Absence of cross-linguistic comparison:** It would be useful to compare Italian with languages that have more complex address systems (e.g., Japanese, Korean).
* **Insufficient attention to individual differences:** Although declared as non-central, they heavily influence pragmatics.

### 5.3. Citation Consistency
The citations are coherent and up to date for 2009, but today they lack more recent references, such as:

* Taguchi (2015) on pragmatic acquisition;
* Bettoni & Di Biase (2015) on processability;
* Ishihara & Cohen (2010) on teaching pragmatics.

---

### 6. Phase 5 – Proposals for Improving the Paper
* **Integrate a controlled experiment:** Two groups—one with an early introduction of politeness, one with a delayed introduction.
* **Measure pragmatic production:** Use role-plays, DCTs (Discourse Completion Tasks), and conversation analysis.
* **Analyze native speaker input:** Utilize a corpus of native/non-native interactions to quantify the level of simplification.
* **Consider socio-cultural variables:** Track age, gender, migratory context, and education level.
* **Propose concrete teaching materials:** Provide specific activities, syllabus sequences, and assessment rubrics.

### 7.3. Predictive Models

### 8. Conclusion
Acquisitional didactics represents a scientifically grounded and pedagogically innovative approach. The paper by Nuzzo & Rastelli shows that linguistic politeness is an ideal domain to test its limits: it is complex, late-acquired, and culturally sensitive, yet indispensable for social integration.

Their thesis—teaching politeness right from the beginning—is compelling, but it requires more robust empirical support. Future outlooks include experimental studies, pragmalinguistic corpora, and integration with advanced technologies.

### 4. Method
**Text Generation**
The copilot model receives a complex, highly structured academic prompt.
Although the prompt includes formal instructions (sections, phases, references, diagrams), it does not provide any explicit epistemic‑verification mechanism: the model is not instructed to check the truth of claims, validate sources, or perform factual grounding.
The constraints are stylistic and structural, not epistemic.

External Validation  
-The generated text is sent to MarCognity‑AI. An epistemic analysis protocol is applied based on:

-Identification of claims

-Assessment of their verifiability

-Detection of omissions

-Classification of epistemic fractures

-Production of a structured report (CLAIM → STATUS → REASON)

Analysis of Results  
The analysis focuses on internal consistency, not medical content.
No qualitative judgments are made about the models.
The goal is to document the observed epistemic behavior.

### 6. Discussion

The test shows that an autoregressive model can produce a complex, well-structured and theoretically plausible text, but without guaranteeing epistemic transparency. External validation highlights unsupported claims, unverifiable quotes and undocumented assumptions.
This makes the Epistemic Boundary evident: the fracture between the linguistic quality of the text and its actual verifiability.
Generation and validation are therefore distinct and complementary processes.

### 7. Conclusion
The test demonstrates that the formal coherence of a text generated by a linguistic model does not imply its epistemic solidity. External validation allows you to identify limits, omissions and unverifiable claims, increasing the transparency of the process.
The combination of language generation and epistemic supervision is essential to evaluate the robustness of content produced by autoregressive models.
